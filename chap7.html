      The graphs of linear functions, quadratic functions, power functions, and exponentials all have a characteristic shape. On the other hand, the family of polynomial functions has graphs that represent a huge variety of different shapes.

      Ever since Gutenberg's invention of movable type in 1455, artists and printers have been interested in the design of pleasing and practical fonts. In 1525, Albrecht Durer published On the Just Shaping of Letters, which set forth a system of rules for the geometric construction of Roman capitals. The letters shown above are examples of Durer's font. Until the twentieth century, a ruler and compass were the only practical design tools, and consequently straight lines and circular arcs were the only geometric objects that could be accurately reproduced.

      With the advent of computers, however, complex curves and surfaces, such as the smooth contours of modern cars, can be defined precisely and reproduced in metal or plastic. In fact, it was the French automobile engineer Pierre BÃ©zier who in the 1960s developed a new design tool based on polynomials. BÃ©zier curves are widely used today in all fields of design, from technical plans and blueprints to the most creative artistic endeavors.

      The study of BÃ©zier curves falls under the general topic of curve fitting, but these curves do not really have a scientific purpose. A scientist would not use BÃ©zier curves to fit a function to data. Rather, BÃ©zier curves have more of an artistic purpose. Computer programs like Illustrator, Freehand, and CorelDrawâ¢ use cubic BÃ©zier curves. The PostScript printer language and Type 1 fonts also use cubic BÃ©zier curves, and TrueType fonts use quadratic BÃ©zier curves.

      Investigation11BÃ©zier Curves

      A BÃ©zier curve is actually a sequence of short curves pieced together. Each piece has two endpoints and (for nonlinear curves) at least one control point. The control points do not lie on the curve itself, but they determine its shape. Two polynomials define the curve, one for the \(x\)-coordinate and one for the \(y\)-coordinate.
