The natural logarithm of \(x\) is the exponent to which \(e\) must be raised to produce \(x\). For example, the natural logarithm of \(10\), or \(\ln 10\), is the solution of the equation \begin{equation*}e^y = 10\end{equation*} You can verify on your calculator that \begin{equation*}e^{\alert{2.3}} ~~ \approx 10 \text{ or } ~~ \ln 10 \approx \alert{2.3}\end{equation*} In general, natural logs obey the same conversion formulas that work for logs to other bases.
