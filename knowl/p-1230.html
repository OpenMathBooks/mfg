You may find it helpful to visualize completing the square geometrically. We can think of the expression \(x^2 + 2px\) as the area of a rectangle with dimensions \(x\) and \(x + 2p\). For example, the rectangle with length \(x + 10\) and width \(x\) has area \(x(x + 10) = x^2 + 10x\), as shown in Figure 6.22a. We would like to cut the rectangle into pieces and rearrange them so that we can make a square. In Figure 6.22b, we move half of the \(x\)-term so that each side of the square has length \(x + 5\) (note that \(p = \frac{1}{2} (10) = 5\)), and in Figure 6.22c we see that the missing corner piece has area \(p^2 = 5^2 = 25\).
