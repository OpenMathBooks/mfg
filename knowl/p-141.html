Solutions of the equation \(P(x) = 0\) are called zeros of the polynomial \(P\). In Example 1.23, we graphed the cubic polynomial \(P(x) = x^3 − 4x\). Its \(x\)-intercepts are the solutions of the equation \(x^3 − 4x = 0\), which we can solve by factoring the polynomial \(P(x)\). \begin{align*} x^3 − 4x \amp= 0 \\ x(x − 2)(x + 2) \amp= 0 \end{align*} The zeros of \(P\) are \(0\), \(2\), and \(−2\). Each zero of \(P\) corresponds to a factor of \(P(x)\). This result suggests the following theorem, which holds for any polynomial \(P\).
