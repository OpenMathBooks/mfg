All these parabolas share certain features. The graph has either a highest point (if the parabola opens downward, as in Figure 6.3.1a) or a lowest point (if the parabola opens upward, as in Figure 6.3.1b). This high or low point is called the vertex of the graph. The parabola is symmetric about a vertical line, called the axis of symmetry, that runs through the vertex. The \(y\)-intercept is the point where the parabola intersects the \(y\)-axis. The graph of a quadratic function always has exactly one \(y\)-intercept. However, the graph may cross the \(x\)-axis at one point, at two points, or not at all. Points where the parabola intersects the \(x\)-axis are called the \(x\)-intercepts. If there are two \(x\)-intercepts, they are equidistant from the axis of symmetry.
