To solve the equation graphically, graph the two functions \begin{equation*} Y_1=\frac{6}{x}+1 \hphantom{space}\text{and}\hphantom{space}Y_2=\frac{1}{x+2} \end{equation*} in the window \begin{align*} \text{Xmin} \amp = -4.7 \amp\amp \text{Xmax} = 4.7 \\ \text{Ymin} \amp = -10 \amp\amp \text{Ymax} = 10 \end{align*} as shown in Figure 7.92a. We see that the first graph has an asymptote at \(x = 0\), and the second graph has one at \(x = −2\). It appears that the two graphs may intersect in the third quadrant, around \(x = −3\). To investigate further, we change the window settings to \begin{align*} \text{Xmin} \amp = -4.55 \amp\amp \text{Xmax} = -2.2 \\ \text{Ymin} \amp = -1.3 \amp\amp \text{Ymax} = -0.3 \end{align*} to obtain the close-up view shown in Figure 7.92b. In this window, we can see that the graphs intersect in two distinct points, and by using the Trace we find that their \(x\)-coordinates are \(x = −3\) and \(x = −4\).
