We can use number lines to solve equations such as \begin{equation*}\abs{3x − 6} = 9\end{equation*} First, factor out the coefficient of \(x\), to get \(\abs{3(x − 2)} = 9\). Because of the multiplicative property of the absolute value, namely that \(\abs{ab} = \abs{a}\abs{b}\), we can write the left side as \begin{align*} \abs{3}\abs{x − 2} \amp = 9 \\ 3\abs{x − 2} \amp= 9 \amp\amp \text{Divide both sides by 3.}\\ \abs{x − 2} \amp = 3 \end{align*} which tells us that the distance between \(x\) and \(2\) is \(3\) units, so \(x = −1\) or \(x = 5\).
