Step 1:

Rani's speed in still water:

 \(s\)

Step 2:

We must write two equations using the variables \(r\) and \(s\). First, organize the information into a table. When Rani travels downstream, the current in the river helps her, so her effective speed is \(r+s\). When she travels upstream she is fighting the current, so her speed is actually \(r-s\).

Using the formula \(\text{rate} \times \text{time} = \text{distance}\), we write one equation describing Rani's journey downstream, and a second equation for the journey upstream. \begin{align*} (r + s) \cdot 45 \amp = 6000 \\ (r − s) \cdot 45 \amp = 4800 \end{align*} Apply the distributive law to write each equation in standard form. \begin{align*} 45r + 45s \amp = 6000 \amp\amp(1) \\ 45r − 45s \amp = 4800 \amp\amp(2) \end{align*}

Step 3:

To solve the system, we eliminate the variable \(s\) by adding the two equations vertically.

We now have an equation in one variable only, which we can solve for \(r\). \begin{align*} 90r \amp = 10,800\amp\amp\text{Divide both sides by 90.} \\ r \amp = 120 \end{align*} To solve for \(s\) we substitute \(r = 120\) into any previous equation involving both \(r\) and \(s\). We will use Equation (1). \begin{align*} 45(120) + 45s\amp = 6000 \amp\amp\text{Simplify the left side.} \\ 5400 + 45s \amp = 6000 \amp\amp\text{Subtract 5400 from both sides.} \\ 45s \amp = 600 \amp\amp\text{Divide both sides by 45; reduce.} \\ s\amp = \frac{40}{3} \end{align*}

Step 4:

The speed of the current is \(\frac{40}{3}\), or \(13\frac{1}{3} \) meters per minute, and Rani's speed in still water is \(120\) meters per minute.
