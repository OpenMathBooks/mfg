SolutionA.39.1

We choose some values for \(x\), say, \(-2\), \(0\), and \(1\). Substitute these \(x\)-values into the equation to find a corresponding \(y\)-value for each. \begin{align*} \amp\text{When }x=−2,\amp\amp y = 2(\alert{−2}) + 3 =−1 \\ \amp\text{When }x=0,\amp\amp y = 2(\alert{0}) + 3 =3 \\ \amp\text{When }x=1,\amp\amp y = 2(\alert{1}) + 3 =5 \end{align*} Thus, the ordered pairs \((−2, −1)\), \((0, 3)\), and \((1, 5)\) are three solutions of \(y = 2x + 3\). We can also substitute values for \(y\). For example, if we let \(y = \alert{10}\), we have \begin{equation*}\alert{10} = 2x + 3\end{equation*} Solving this equation for \(x\), we find \(7 = 2x\), or \(x = 3.5\). This means that the ordered pair \((3.5, 10)\) is another solution of the equation \(y = 2x + 3\).
